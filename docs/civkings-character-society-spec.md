# CivKings — Character & Society Spec (2026-07-16)

**Status:** Design source-of-truth for the character layer, dynasty/capital layer, political
economy, intrigue, and event system. Companion to `civkings-deep-systems-spec.md`.
**This document SUPERSEDES Part 4 (CK layer) of the deep-systems spec**, and revises its
timeline/setting assumptions (Parts 0 and 2 assumed an ancient→modern historical sweep; the
game is now set in a fictional 1900 with a narrowed ~century arc — the tech tree spans that
industrial century instead).

All decisions below were locked one-by-one in a brainstorming session with the owner
(13 questions; decision log at the end).

---

## 1. Vision & Thesis

CivKings is a 4X / Crusader-Kings hybrid where **the family is the firm is the state**.
You play the mortal head of a Great House across generations, in a fictional world's
industrial age.

**The thesis, built into the mechanics:** the profit motive grinds everyone. Workers die in
your mines; your own family cracks under the weight of the machine they sit atop. The game's
incentive gradient pushes the player toward cruelty — and the tragedy at the top (stress,
scandal, breakdowns, alienated heirs) is the emergent price of following it. Nobody wins the
game of capital cleanly; the player only decides who pays. This is never flavor text: every
harm is generated by real mechanics (Section 5).

---

## 2. The World & The Frame

- **Setting:** fictional world, year **1900-equivalent**. Electricity, rail, steel,
  telegraphs, early automobiles at start; radio, flight, and worse arrive via the tech tree.
  No real nations. The map is held by **Great Houses**: aristocratic dynasties who ARE the
  industrial powers — titles AND ownership of the mines, mills, rail, and banks.
  Crown and capital, fused.
- **Dynasty-first:** you play the House head, a mortal character. On death you become the
  heir, as shaped (or ruined) by the education you gave them.
  **Dynasty extinction = game over.** Losing to revolution = game over, *unless* the House
  transformed with it (Section 5.3).
- **Time scale:** ~1–2 years per turn. A full game spans roughly a century — three to four
  generations, the whole arc of an industrial age.
- **Realm structure — optional vassalage:** the first cities are the player's **personal
  domain** (hard cap; the tall, directly-managed core). Beyond the cap, cities are
  **enfeoffed** to characters as vassal-Directors, or the player stays tall. Wide play means
  managing *people*, not spreadsheets.

## 3. Characters: Living Dispositions

### 3.1 Population & tiered simulation
- **Full CK-style population.** Every Great House has a real simulated family tree —
  spouses, children, cousins, bastards — plus courtiers, executives, and agents.
  Hundreds to 1000+ living characters.
- **Tiered simulation (LOD)** makes this feasible in Python:
  - **Tier 0 (everyone, every turn, cheap/vectorizable):** aging, mortality, disposition
    drift from global pressures, fertility/births.
  - **Tier 1 (relevant characters, full logic):** rulers, heirs, council members, Directors,
    Commanders, scheme participants, the player's court. These run schemes, opinion updates,
    stress, event generation.
  - **Promotion is seamless:** any character who inherits, is hired, marries into relevance,
    or becomes a scheme target wakes into Tier 1 with their (cheaply-maintained) state intact.
  - This mirrors CK3's own under-the-hood approach; it is not faking the simulation.

### 3.2 Attributes (6)
**Statecraft, Command, Industry, Intrigue, Science, Resolve.**
Attributes scale every job a character holds (Section 4.3) and grow slowly through jobs and
Focus (Section 3.6).

### 3.3 Trait spectrums — three families of paired opposites
Traits are NOT binary stamps. Each pair is a value on **−100..+100**; named traits are
**thresholds** (past ±50 the character *is* e.g. "Compassionate"; past ±80 an extreme label,
e.g. "Bleeding Heart"). The value moves with life events → characters have arcs.

**Temperament (~15 pairs):**
Bold↔Craven, Cruel↔Compassionate, Ambitious↔Content, Honest↔Deceitful,
Temperate↔Hedonist, Diligent↔Slothful, Forgiving↔Vengeful, Generous↔Greedy,
Gregarious↔Reclusive, Humble↔Arrogant, Patient↔Impulsive, Stoic↔Volatile,
Trusting↔Paranoid, Romantic↔Cold, Principled↔Pragmatic.

**Conviction (~9 pairs) — the novel 1900 ideological layer:**
Traditionalist↔Modernist, Labor↔Capital, Pious↔Secular, Militarist↔Pacifist,
Nationalist↔Cosmopolitan, Autocrat↔Constitutionalist, Protectionist↔Free-Trader,
Paternalist↔Meritocrat, Preservationist↔Extractionist.
Convictions drive realm friction, appointments, marriage politics, and the revolution
mechanic (Section 5.3).

**Bloodline (congenital; inherited, blendable, breedable):**
Brilliant↔Dull, Robust↔Sickly, Magnetic↔Repellent, plus fertility and longevity lines.
Bloodline spectrums blend at conception with mutation — Houses can (and do) breed for talent.

### 3.4 Drift, stress, and breaks
- **Drift:** life events push the sliders. Witnessing a mine collapse you ordered ignored
  drifts a character toward Callous *or* snaps them toward Reformist (weighted by existing
  temperament). Your heir at 40 is not who they were at 15.
- **Stress:** accrues when a character acts against their Temperament OR their Convictions
  (a Compassionate ruler suppressing a strike; a Labor-sympathetic Director running the
  squeeze).
- **Mental breaks:** at stress thresholds, a break fires → the character adopts a **coping
  vice** (drink, gambling, cruelty, seclusion...) as a lasting trait, with mechanical and
  social costs.

### 3.5 Persona vs Private Self
Every character has a **Public Persona** (reputation; what society believes) and a **Private
Self** (true spectrum values). The gap is where **secrets** live — affairs, hidden
convictions, crimes, the true ledger of an "accident." Secrets fuel the intrigue economy
(Section 6): they can be discovered, held, sold, or detonated in the tabloids.

### 3.6 Growth over a lifetime
- **Childhood:** the player appoints a **guardian** per child (guardian traits/convictions
  rub off — hand your heir to a Radical tutor and see what comes home) and picks an
  **education track** mapped to one of the six attributes. Outcome quality depends on
  guardian skill + the child's Bloodline.
- **Adulthood:** each adult holds one **Focus** (one per attribute line, e.g. Industry →
  "Captain of Industry", Intrigue → "The Long Game"). A Focus grants a small passive, a
  themed periodic event stream, and slow attribute growth. Changing Focus is free but resets
  progress. Deliberately CK-lite: no sprawling perk trees.

## 4. Dynasty & Capital: The Family Is the Firm

### 4.1 Shares
House wealth is **ownership**: shares in the House's enterprises (mines, mills, rail,
banks). Shares pay dividends, carry board votes, and can be traded, dowered, bequeathed,
or raided.

### 4.2 Succession — title and capital split differently
- The **title** (House headship; the character the player controls next) follows succession
  law.
- The **capital partitions**: shares divide among heirs by law and testament.
- Result: the succession crisis is not map fragmentation — it is inheriting the headship
  while siblings hold 40% of your own company. Disgruntled heirs become **shareholder
  rivals**: voting against you, selling to enemies, backing rival branches.
- The real succession game happens **before the death**: buy siblings out, marry them off,
  seat them on the board, or disinherit and accept the hatred.

### 4.3 Marriage as merger
Every betrothal is a **negotiated contract**:
- alliance (mutual defense) + relationship line,
- dowry in cash or **shares** (marrying into House Vayne may cost 5% of your steelworks —
  or win 5% of theirs),
- board seats, exclusivity deals (their coal feeds your foundries),
- matrilineal/patrilineal — which House the children (and their inheritance claims) belong to.
Bloodline genetics ride along: an Industry-genius bride is worth real percentage points.
Patient players can marry into controlling interest of a rival House over two generations.
**Courtship pressure runs through the existing scheme verbs** (sway the patriarch, seduce
the heir, scandalize the House to crash their asking price) — no separate courtship sub-loop.

### 4.4 The org chart — characters bound to the map
This is the load-bearing fusion wire (the old code's single stewardship→tax hook becomes a
whole layer):
- **Council seats** (realm-wide): Board Chairman, Chief Engineer, Head of Security, Master
  of the Press, etc. Holder's attribute → realm modifier + unlocked seat actions.
- **Director** per city (governor): their **Industry** scales city production; their
  **Convictions** shape outcomes (Reformist Director: less unrest, less profit; Callous:
  more of both, plus accident rate); their **loyalty** is real — the bitter or bought can
  embezzle, defect during a takeover, or hand the city to revolutionaries.
- **Commander** per army: **Command** drives combat; defeat and glory-starvation feed
  stress and drift.
- Every posting is a promise and a risk: the talented demand seats; the passed-over grow
  bitter. The family tree IS the org chart.

## 5. Political Economy: The Machine That Grinds

### 5.1 The Squeeze (extraction dial)
Every enterprise has an **extraction dial** — wages, hours, safety spending, conditions.
- Dial up → more production and dividends **now**, and the dial **mechanically generates
  harm**: industrial accidents (named pops die; characters can be maimed), disease, unrest,
  and disposition drift in witnesses — including the player's family.
- Dial down → workers live better; rival Houses who ARE squeezing outproduce and outbid you.
- The incentive gradient pushes toward cruelty **by design**.
- **The cost climbs the ladder:** suppressing a strike stresses the ruler who signs the
  order; the heir who tours the mines drifts Reformist or Callous; scandal, breakdowns, and
  coping vices are the elite's share of the grind.

### 5.2 Labor as a simulated force
- Squeezed populations **organize**: unrest crystallizes into unions, strikes, sabotage,
  and city-level movements.
- Movements have **leaders — real characters** (Tier-1 promoted): a firebrand organizer can
  be swayed, bought, framed, or martyred — and martyrdom is how movements go regional.
- A world-wide **ideological tide** rises across the century: reformism → socialism →
  revolutionary pressure, accelerated by every atrocity ANY House commits anywhere.

### 5.3 Revolution — and riding the wave
- When a House's legitimacy craters while the movement peaks: **uprising**. Losing = game
  over. The wall.
- **Transformation path:** a ruler whose Convictions have *genuinely* drifted Labor-ward
  (Persona alone isn't believed — the Persona/Private-Self gap is checked) can break with
  the Great Houses: concede the enterprises, arm the strikers, transform. The player
  survives as something new (People's Chairman / constitutional figurehead): shares become
  political capital, rival Houses become class enemies, the endgame changes shape.
- Ideology is a **strategy axis**, not a doom meter.

## 6. Intrigue: The Knife and the Newspaper

Everyday scheming is **leverage**, not poison:
- **Expose** — feed a rival's secret to the tabloids (Master of the Press weaponizes this);
  craters Persona, legitimacy, and marriage-market value.
- **Blackmail** — hold the secret; extort shares, votes, favors.
- **Sabotage** — engineered "accidents" at rival works; deniable, ugly, and it kills THEIR
  workers (the machine grinds even as a weapon).
- **Sway / Seduce / Compromise** — build or fabricate leverage on a person; also the
  courtship-pressure verbs (Section 4.3).
- **Hostile takeover** — the signature bloodless kill: quietly buy a rival House's shares
  via siblings, widows, and disgruntled heirs until you own their company out from under
  them. A House can be ended without a shot. (Our setting's answer to CK's claim-war.)
- **Assassination** — exists, but as a **conspiracy**: hired men, staged accidents,
  anarchist patsies. Expensive; requires co-conspirators (each a betrayal risk); exposure
  is the nuclear scandal (legitimacy collapse, Houses close ranks against you, possible
  trial). Everyone fears it; almost no one dares it.

## 7. Events: Systemic Spine, Authored Punch

- **The simulation generates every situation** — accidents, strikes, betrayals, breakdowns,
  proposals, exposés. All are real state changes; never dice-roll flavor.
- Each situation renders through a **template pool**: several hand-written text variants per
  situation type, slotted with names, traits, and history; choices are generated from what
  those specific characters can actually do.
- **~50–100 authored signature chains** for the thematic gut-punches: the mine disaster
  inquiry, the heir's radicalization, the tabloid war, the revolution's ultimatum.
- **Templates and chains are data files** — content ships incrementally across many small
  CynCo briefs rather than one monolith.

## 8. Engineering Notes

- Language/runtime: existing Python/pygame codebase. Tier-0 population math should be flat
  arrays / dicts updated in bulk; Tier-1 logic object-oriented per character.
- All numeric tunables (drift rates, stress thresholds, dial curves, tide pacing) live as
  named constants grouped per system, per the deep-systems spec's tuning convention.
- Event templates/chains: data files (JSON or Python data modules) loaded at start; the
  event engine is generic.
- Integration: this layer plugs into the deep-systems spec's economy via the extraction
  dial (modifies city yields + unrest), Directors (city multipliers), Commanders (combat),
  and legitimacy (victory gates). Mission decomposition for Waves C+ (M34-M40 and beyond)
  must be re-derived from THIS document.
- ALL implementation lands via CynCo Appendix-B mission briefs. No direct edits to game
  code by the planning assistant — ever.
